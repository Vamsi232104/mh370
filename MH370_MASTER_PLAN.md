## 6. Fitness Terminology — What Everything Means

**You must understand this before the exercise section makes sense.**

### Reps (Repetitions)
One complete movement of an exercise — up and back down.
For bench press: push bar up + lower bar down = 1 rep.
"8–10 reps" means you do the movement 8 to 10 times in a row without stopping.

### Sets
A group of reps done in a row, followed by rest.
"4 sets" means you do the exercise 4 separate times with rest in between.

### What "4 × 8–10" means
Do 8–10 reps → rest 2–3 minutes → do 8–10 reps again → rest → again → rest → again.
That is 4 sets. You have now done 32–40 total reps for that exercise.

### Rest Between Sets
- Compound exercises (bench press, squat, deadlift, row): **2–3 minutes**
- Isolation exercises (lateral raises, curls, pushdowns): **60–90 seconds**
Why? Big compound lifts need more recovery. Small isolation lifts need less.

### Progressive Overload
The single most important concept in all of fitness.
Every week, try to do either more weight or more reps than last week. Even 1 extra rep counts.
Your body only grows when it is forced to adapt to something harder than before.

### Compound Exercise
An exercise that uses multiple muscle groups and multiple joints at once.
Examples: bench press, squat, deadlift, overhead press, barbell row.
These are the most important exercises. Always done first in the session.

### Isolation Exercise
An exercise that targets one specific muscle with one joint moving.
Examples: lateral raises, bicep curls, tricep pushdowns, leg curls.
Done last in the session, after compound work.

### Failure
The point where you cannot do one more rep with correct form.
"Training to failure" means going until the muscle gives out.
As a beginner: stop 1–2 reps before failure. Learn form first.

### Warm-Up Set
Before your first working set of any heavy exercise, do 1 set with 40–50% of your working weight.
This warms the muscle and practices the movement before real load is added.

### PUSH DAY — Monday & Thursday
**Total session: 65 minutes**
**What push day builds:** Chest thickness. Shoulder roundness. Tricep definition. The silhouette that changes how shirts fit.

---

#### 1. Barbell Bench Press
**What it is:** You lie on your back on a flat bench and push a weighted barbell up away from your chest.

**Why it's in your plan:** The single best exercise for chest mass. There is no substitute. This is the foundation of your push day. Do not skip this, do not rush it. Every kilogram you add here shows on your chest.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie flat on the bench. Your eyes should be directly under the barbell.
2. Grip the bar slightly wider than your shoulders. Wrap your thumbs fully around the bar.
3. Plant your feet flat on the floor. Squeeze your shoulder blades together and push them into the bench.
4. Lift the bar off the rack and hold it directly above your chest with arms fully extended.
5. Lower the bar slowly (2–3 seconds) until it gently touches the middle of your chest.
6. Press the bar up explosively until arms are straight. Breathe out as you push up.

**Most common mistake:** Flaring elbows out at 90 degrees from your body. This destroys your shoulder joints over time. Keep elbows at 45–75 degrees from your torso.

**Starting weight:** Just the bar (20 kg). For your first 2 weeks, use only the bar to learn the movement. Add weight only when form is perfect.

**YouTube:** [Watch tutorial](https://www.youtube.com/results?search_query=barbell+bench+press+proper+form+beginners+chest)

---

#### 2. Overhead Press (Barbell)
**What it is:** You stand and press a barbell from shoulder height straight up above your head.

**Why it's in your plan:** Builds the front and side of the shoulders (the "cap" that makes shoulders look round and wide). Also builds triceps and upper chest. One of the best upper body exercises for a skinny guy because it adds width and thickness to the top half of your body.

**How to do it:**
1. Stand with feet shoulder-width apart. Hold the bar at shoulder height with hands slightly wider than shoulders.
2. The bar should rest on your upper chest/front of shoulders before each rep.
3. Look straight ahead. Take a breath and brace your core (tighten your stomach like you're about to get punched).
4. Press the bar straight up. As it clears your head, push your head slightly forward so it ends directly over your head.
5. Lower the bar back to shoulder height slowly. Repeat.

**Most common mistake:** Leaning back excessively to get the bar up. This dumps the work into your lower back. Keep your body vertical. If you're leaning back, the weight is too heavy.

**Starting weight:** 20 kg (empty bar). Overhead press is harder than it looks. Start with just the bar.

**YouTube:** [Watch tutorial](https://www.youtube.com/results?search_query=barbell+overhead+press+standing+form+beginners)

---

#### 3. Incline Dumbbell Press
**What it is:** Same as bench press but the bench is angled upward (30–45 degrees) and you use two separate dumbbells instead of a barbell.

**Why it's in your plan:** Targets the upper chest specifically. The upper chest is what gives you the "full chest" look from the front — the line that runs from your armpit toward your collar. Without incline work, the upper chest stays flat even when the rest develops.

**How to do it:**
1. Set the bench to 30–45 degrees incline. Sit down with a dumbbell on each knee.
2. Kick the dumbbells up one at a time as you lie back — bring your knee up to help get them in position.
3. Hold the dumbbells at chest level with palms facing forward.
4. Press both dumbbells up together until arms are extended above your upper chest.
5. Lower slowly back to start. Control the movement — don't just let them drop.

**Most common mistake:** Setting the bench too steep (above 45 degrees). This shifts the work to your shoulders and away from your chest.

**Starting weight:** 8–10 kg dumbbells per hand. Dumbbells feel unstable at first. Go lighter to learn balance before adding weight.

**YouTube:** [Watch tutorial](https://www.youtube.com/results?search_query=incline+dumbbell+press+proper+form+upper+chest)

---

#### 4. Lateral Raises (Dumbbell)
**What it is:** You stand and raise dumbbells out to the side like wings spreading.

**Why it's in your plan:** Isolates the lateral (side) head of the shoulder. This is what creates shoulder width — the "boulder shoulder" look. As a skinny guy, this exercise directly changes how wide you look from the front.

**How to do it:**
1. Stand straight, feet shoulder-width. Hold a dumbbell in each hand at your sides.
2. Keep a very slight bend in your elbows throughout.
3. Raise both arms out to the side slowly until they are level with your shoulders. Your body should form a T-shape.
4. Lower them slowly back down (3 seconds down). Do not let gravity do it for you.
5. Do not shrug your shoulders up as you raise. Keep them pressed down.

**Most common mistake:** Using too heavy a weight and swinging the body to help. Use light weight. The movement should be pure arm raising — no momentum, no body rocking.

**Starting weight:** 5–6 kg dumbbells. This exercise is always done lighter than you expect.

**YouTube:** [Watch tutorial](https://www.youtube.com/results?search_query=dumbbell+lateral+raises+proper+form+no+momentum)

---

#### 5. Tricep Pushdown (Cable Machine)
**What it is:** You stand at a cable machine and push a handle or rope downward by straightening your elbows.

**Why it's in your plan:** The tricep makes up 2/3 of your upper arm size. If you want bigger arms, triceps are more important than biceps. The pushdown isolates the tricep effectively without needing complex positioning.

**How to do it:**
1. Attach a rope or straight bar to the cable machine at the top pulley.
2. Stand facing the machine. Hold the attachment with both hands, elbows at your sides.
3. Keep your elbows pinned to your sides — they should not move during the exercise.
4. Push the attachment straight down by extending your arms fully. Squeeze the tricep at the bottom.
5. Let the cable pull your hands back up slowly to the start position. Control it.

**Most common mistake:** Letting the elbows flare out or drift forward. If elbows move, the work shifts to your back and shoulders. Pin them to your sides throughout.

**Starting weight:** Set cable to a moderate weight where you can feel the tricep working without your elbows moving. Adjust from there.

**YouTube:** [Watch tutorial](https://www.youtube.com/results?search_query=cable+tricep+pushdown+rope+proper+form+elbows)

---

#### 6. Overhead Tricep Extension (Cable)
**What it is:** You hold a cable attachment behind your head and extend your arms upward to straighten them.

**Why it's in your plan:** Targets the long head of the tricep — the largest portion that runs down the back of the arm. This is what gives the arm that thick appearance from behind. The pushdown targets a different head. Both are needed for complete tricep development.

**How to do it:**
1. Attach a rope to the low cable pulley. Face away from the machine.
2. Hold the rope with both hands behind your head. Elbows pointing toward the ceiling.
3. Your upper arms stay vertical and still. Only your forearms move.
4. Extend your arms upward until fully straight. Squeeze at the top.
5. Lower the rope slowly back behind your head. Feel the stretch in the tricep.

**Most common mistake:** Letting the upper arms fall forward. Keep them pointing straight up. If they drift forward, the stretch disappears and the long head is no longer loaded.

**Starting weight:** Lighter than pushdown. The position feels unfamiliar. Start light.

**YouTube:** [Watch tutorial](https://www.youtube.com/results?search_query=overhead+cable+tricep+extension+long+head+form)

### PULL DAY — Tuesday & Friday
**Total session: 65 min (Tuesday) / 75 min (Friday with deadlift)**
**What pull day builds:** Back width. Back thickness. Bicep peak. Forearms. The V-taper silhouette seen from behind. The posture that makes you stand and sit differently.

---

#### 1. Barbell Bent-Over Row
**What it is:** You hold a barbell and bend forward, then row the bar up toward your stomach.

**Why it's in your plan:** Builds back thickness and the "width" of your lats. One of the most effective back exercises. Also strengthens your lower back, which protects you in all other exercises.

**How to do it:**
1. Stand with feet shoulder-width. Hold the bar with an overhand grip slightly wider than shoulders.
2. Bend at the hips until your torso is at roughly 45 degrees to the floor. Keep your back flat — not rounded.
3. Let the bar hang straight down from your hands.
4. Pull the bar up toward your lower chest/upper stomach. Lead with your elbows, not your hands.
5. Squeeze your shoulder blades together at the top. Lower the bar slowly.

**Most common mistake:** Rounding the lower back. A rounded lower back under load causes serious injury. Keep your spine neutral and your chest up. If you cannot, the weight is too heavy.

**Starting weight:** 40 kg (bar + small plates). You will feel weak at first. Form over weight always.

**YouTube:** [Watch tutorial](https://www.youtube.com/results?search_query=barbell+bent+over+row+proper+form+back+beginners)

---

#### 2. Lat Pulldown (Wide Grip)
**What it is:** You sit at a cable machine and pull a wide bar down from above to chest level.

**Why it's in your plan:** Targets the latissimus dorsi — the large muscle on the sides of your back that creates the V-taper (the wide-back look). This is the muscle that makes you look broad from the front and back. You cannot get the "wide back" look without training the lats.

**How to do it:**
1. Sit at the lat pulldown machine. Adjust the knee pad so it holds your thighs down.
2. Grip the wide bar with hands beyond shoulder-width. Palms facing away from you.
3. Lean back very slightly (about 15 degrees). No more than that.
4. Pull the bar down to your upper chest. Lead with your elbows — pull them toward your hips.
5. Squeeze at the bottom. Slowly let the bar rise back up until arms are fully extended. Feel the stretch.

**Most common mistake:** Pulling the bar down behind the neck. This strains the cervical spine and adds no benefit. Always pull to the front of your chest.

**Starting weight:** Set the stack to a weight where you can do 10–12 reps with good form — typically 30–50 kg depending on the machine.

**YouTube:** [Watch tutorial](https://www.youtube.com/results?search_query=lat+pulldown+wide+grip+proper+form+lats+V+taper)

---

#### 3. Seated Cable Row
**What it is:** You sit at a cable machine with your legs extended, and row a handle toward your stomach.

**Why it's in your plan:** Builds mid-back thickness — the muscle between your shoulder blades that makes your back look "full" rather than just wide. Also trains your rear delts and traps.

**How to do it:**
1. Sit at the cable row machine. Place your feet on the footpad, legs slightly bent.
2. Lean forward to grip the handle with both hands. Your back should be straight.
3. Sit up straight. This is your starting position — slight lean forward.
4. Pull the handle to your stomach. Lead with your elbows behind you. Squeeze shoulder blades together.
5. Slowly extend your arms back forward. Let your shoulder blades separate at the front to get the full stretch.

**Most common mistake:** Rocking back and forth using your lower back as momentum. Your lower back should not be doing the work. Control the movement with your back muscles.

**YouTube:** [Watch tutorial](https://www.youtube.com/results?search_query=seated+cable+row+proper+form+mid+back+beginners)

---

#### 4. Cable Face Pulls
**What it is:** You pull a cable rope toward your face, splitting the rope apart as you pull.

**Why it's in your plan:** Targets rear delts and external rotators — muscles on the back of the shoulder that most beginners never train. These are critical for shoulder health and posture. Without strong rear delts, heavy pressing causes shoulder injuries over time.

**How to do it:**
1. Set the cable machine to face height. Attach a rope handle.
2. Stand back and hold the rope with both hands, thumbs pointing away from you.
3. Pull the rope directly toward your face, splitting it apart at the end so your hands go to either side of your head.
4. Your elbows should be at shoulder height and flaring out, not dropping down.
5. Return slowly. Keep tension throughout.

**Most common mistake:** Letting your elbows drop below shoulder height. This turns it into a row and loses the rear delt focus. Keep elbows up.

**Starting weight:** Very light. This is a small muscle. 10–15 kg on the cable.

**YouTube:** [Watch tutorial](https://www.youtube.com/results?search_query=cable+face+pulls+rear+delts+proper+form+elbows+high)

---

#### 5. Barbell Curl
**What it is:** You hold a barbell and curl it up toward your chest by bending your elbows.

**Why it's in your plan:** The primary bicep mass builder. The barbell allows you to lift more weight than dumbbells, meaning more stimulus for growth. This is what builds the bicep "peak" you see when the arm is flexed.

**How to do it:**
1. Stand straight. Hold the bar with an underhand grip, hands shoulder-width.
2. Keep your elbows pinned to your sides. They must not move during the curl.
3. Curl the bar up toward your chest by bending your elbows. Squeeze the bicep at the top.
4. Lower the bar slowly — take 3 full seconds to bring it down. Do not let it drop.
5. At the bottom, fully extend your arms before starting the next rep.

**Most common mistake:** Swinging the back to help get the bar up. This means the weight is too heavy. The bicep is doing zero work. Drop the weight and use strict form.

**Starting weight:** 30 kg (bar + small plates). Go light enough that elbows stay locked to your sides.

**YouTube:** [Watch tutorial](https://www.youtube.com/results?search_query=barbell+curl+strict+form+no+swinging+bicep)

---

#### 6. Hammer Curl (Dumbbell)
**What it is:** Same as a bicep curl but with dumbbells held vertically (thumbs up) like a hammer.

**Why it's in your plan:** Targets the brachialis — a muscle that sits underneath the bicep and pushes it up from below when developed. Also builds forearm thickness. The brachialis is what makes the arm look thick from the side rather than just the front.

**How to do it:**
1. Stand with a dumbbell in each hand. Palms facing each other (neutral grip) — like holding a hammer.
2. Keep elbows at your sides.
3. Curl both dumbbells up together (or alternating) until bicep is fully contracted.
4. Lower slowly. Same principle as regular curl but with the neutral grip throughout.

**Most common mistake:** Rotating the wrist to a supinated (palm-up) position during the curl. This turns it into a regular curl. Keep palms facing each other the whole time.

**Starting weight:** 10–12 kg per hand.

**YouTube:** [Watch tutorial](https://www.youtube.com/results?search_query=hammer+curl+neutral+grip+brachialis+forearm+form)

### LEGS DAY — Wednesday & Saturday
**Total session: 75 minutes**
**What leg day builds:** Thigh size and separation. Glute development. Calf muscle. Overall lower body mass (50% of total muscle). And because leg training uses the largest muscles, it releases the most growth hormone — which grows your upper body too.

---

#### 1. Barbell Back Squat
**What it is:** You rest a barbell on your upper back and lower your entire body by bending at the hips and knees, then stand back up.

**Why it's in your plan:** The king of all exercises. Nothing builds legs — or total body mass — like the squat. It trains quads, hamstrings, glutes, core, and lower back simultaneously. For a skinny guy, squats are the fastest way to add mass to the lower half of the body.

**How to do it:**
1. Set the bar on the squat rack at upper chest height. Step under it, rest it across your upper traps (upper back). It should NOT be on your neck.
2. Step back from the rack. Feet shoulder-width or slightly wider, toes turned slightly out.
3. Take a breath, brace your core. Initiate the movement by pushing your hips back and bending your knees simultaneously.
4. Lower until your thighs are at least parallel to the floor. Knees should track over your toes — not caving inward.
5. Drive through your heels to stand back up. Push the floor away from you. Breathe out on the way up.

**Most common mistake:** Letting your knees cave inward on the way up. This is a knee injury waiting to happen. Push your knees outward, in the same direction as your toes.

**Starting weight:** Empty bar (20 kg). Spend your first week learning depth and knee tracking before adding any weight.

**YouTube:** [Watch tutorial](https://www.youtube.com/results?search_query=barbell+back+squat+form+beginners+depth+knees)

---

#### 2. Romanian Deadlift
**What it is:** You hold a barbell and lower it down your legs by hinging at the hips, feeling a deep stretch in your hamstrings, then stand back up.

**Why it's in your plan:** Directly targets the hamstrings (back of thighs) and glutes. Most beginners have severely underdeveloped hamstrings compared to quads. The Romanian deadlift fixes this imbalance and also trains the lower back.

**How to do it:**
1. Hold the bar with an overhand grip at hip width. Stand tall.
2. Keep a slight bend in your knees — this slight bend stays fixed throughout.
3. Push your hips back and lower the bar slowly along your legs (bar stays close to your body).
4. Lower until you feel a strong stretch in your hamstrings — typically bar at mid-shin level.
5. Drive your hips forward to return to standing. Squeeze your glutes at the top.

**Most common mistake:** Bending the knees too much and turning it into a regular deadlift. Keep that slight knee bend fixed. The movement comes entirely from hinging at the hips.

**Starting weight:** 40 kg. This exercise requires feeling the hamstring stretch, not lifting heavy.

**YouTube:** [Watch tutorial](https://www.youtube.com/results?search_query=romanian+deadlift+hamstrings+hip+hinge+form+beginners)

---

#### 3. Leg Press (Machine)
**What it is:** You sit in a machine, push a weighted platform away from you with your legs, then bring it back.

**Why it's in your plan:** Lets you train legs with heavy weight without the balance and technique demands of the squat. Great for building quad volume and for days when squats feel taxing. Also safer for a complete beginner while still building mass.

**How to do it:**
1. Sit in the machine with your back and head against the pad.
2. Place your feet on the platform. Feet shoulder-width, toes slightly out.
3. High foot placement = more glutes and hamstrings. Low foot placement = more quads.
4. Release the safety handles. Slowly lower the platform toward your chest by bending your knees.
5. Stop when knees reach 90 degrees. Push through your entire foot to extend legs back.
6. Do not lock your knees out at the top. Keep a slight bend.

**Most common mistake:** Going down too far and letting your lower back peel off the seat. If your lower back lifts, stop there. That is your maximum range for now.

**Starting weight:** 40–60 kg. The leg press feels easy compared to squats. You will progress faster here.

**YouTube:** [Watch tutorial](https://www.youtube.com/results?search_query=leg+press+machine+proper+form+foot+placement+beginners)

---

#### 4. Leg Curl (Machine)
**What it is:** You lie face-down on a machine and curl the pad up toward your glutes by bending your knees.

**Why it's in your plan:** The only way to isolate the hamstrings without also loading the lower back. Critical for balanced leg development. Without direct hamstring work, quad dominance leads to knee problems over time.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie face-down on the machine. Position the ankle pad just above your heel.
2. Hold the handles under the machine.
3. Curl the pad up toward your glutes by bending your knees. Go as high as you can.
4. Squeeze the hamstring at the top. Lower slowly — take 3 seconds.
5. Do not lift your hips off the pad. If your hips are rising, the weight is too heavy.

**YouTube:** [Watch tutorial](https://www.youtube.com/results?search_query=lying+leg+curl+machine+hamstrings+proper+form)

---

#### 5. Walking Lunges
**What it is:** You step forward into a long stride, lower your back knee toward the floor, then step the back foot forward and repeat.

**Why it's in your plan:** Works each leg independently, which finds and fixes strength imbalances between legs. Also trains balance, coordination, and glutes in a way that machines cannot replicate.

**How to do it:**
1. Stand tall with feet together. You can hold dumbbells or use bodyweight to start.
2. Step one foot forward into a long stride — about 70–80 cm.
3. Lower your back knee toward the floor until your front thigh is parallel to the ground. Front knee stays over your ankle, not beyond your toes.
4. Push through your front heel to stand, bringing your back foot forward to stand.
5. Repeat with the opposite leg. This is walking — each step is one rep.

**Most common mistake:** Front knee tracking past your toes. Keep your shin relatively vertical on the front leg. Short step = knee past toe. Long step = knee stays behind toe.

**Starting weight:** Bodyweight only for the first 2 weeks. Add dumbbells once movement is natural.

**YouTube:** [Watch tutorial](https://www.youtube.com/results?search_query=walking+lunges+proper+form+knee+tracking+beginners)

#### 6. Standing Calf Raises
**What it is:** You stand on a raised surface and push up onto your toes, then lower your heels below the surface level.

**Why it's in your plan:** Calves are visible from the back when someone looks at you. Developed calves are also a marker of athletic physique. The gastrocnemius (the prominent calf muscle) is only fully activated when the knee is straight — which is why standing calf raises are for this muscle specifically.

**How to do it:**
1. Stand on a calf raise machine or on a step with your heels hanging off the edge.
2. Push up onto your toes as high as possible. Full contraction — squeeze at the top.
3. Lower your heels as far below the step level as possible. Full stretch.
4. The full range (stretch to squeeze) is what makes calves grow. Partial reps do almost nothing.
5. Pause 1 second at the bottom stretch. Pause 1 second at the top squeeze.

**Most common mistake:** Partial range of motion — only going halfway down. Calves need a full stretch to get the growth signal. Lower all the way down every rep.

**YouTube:** [Watch tutorial](https://www.youtube.com/results?search_query=standing+calf+raises+full+range+of+motion+squeeze+stretch)

### Mutton — Full Profile

**What it is:** Meat from sheep or goat. Very common in Mauritius. Available as curry, chops, mince, or leg cuts.

**Protein:** ~26g per 100g (similar to beef)

**Creatine:** Similar to beef — makes it the best beef alternative for push days and deadlift days.

**How to cook simply:** Mutton curry, mutton mince cooked with spices, grilled mutton chops.

**Which days to use it:** Monday (Push), Friday (Pull/deadlift). These are the beef days. Mutton substitutes directly.

**Not recommended for:** Replacing chicken on pull or leg days — the higher fat content slows digestion during high-volume sessions.

### Carbohydrate Substitutes

| Original | Alternative | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| White rice | Regular potato | Similar calories, slightly different glycemic index |
| White rice | Bread (2–3 slices) | Works in a pinch. Not as clean but acceptable |
| Sweet potato | Pumpkin | Available in Mauritius. Similar potassium content |
| Oats | Corn flakes | Lower fiber, but works for pre-workout if oats are out |
| Pasta | Extra rice | Use on Thursday if no pasta — adjust quantity for same calories |

### When No Alternatives Are Available
If you genuinely cannot get the right protein on a given day: eggs are always the backup. 6 large eggs = approximately 36g protein. Combine with whatever carb source is available. This is never the plan — it is the emergency fallback.
